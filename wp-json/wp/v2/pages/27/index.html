At first glance, opals seem to be quite ordinary gemstones. Mineraloids formed from silica, they are mined from sediment, clay and rock in a few countries such as Australia, Mexico and Ethiopia. Unlike with other minerals, no heavy machinery is required. If he or she knows where to look, a single prospector can excavate them with a few tools and a lot of dedication.<\/p>\n

When found, they are covered in dirt, dust and stone. Yet, even after having been washed and refined, they can still appear to be rather dull\u2014that is, until they are pierced with light. Only then do they emanate their true beauty; scintillating specks of colour emerging from deep within. Suddenly, the seemingly lackluster becomes wonderful.<\/p>\n

The future must stay bright<\/h1>\n

Everywhere around the world, the quality of public education is being plagued by serious human failings, disproportionately impacting the most vulnerable. Underfunding, discrimination, censorship, student safety, overcrowding, high drop-out rates and unequal access to education have become the norm in too many countries.<\/h6>\n
Here in Ontario, the conditions created by the pandemic and the recent series of public sector cuts are amplifying the issues students have been facing, leaving them to face the future less prepared than ever before.<\/h6>\n
We build socially-responsible educational solutions that genuinely prepare students for the future they will build.<\/h5>\n

Mindset comes first<\/h1>\n

Learning to learn is a complex and ongoing process that includes learning to think, to analyze and to organize. It also includes learning to fail and to embrace difficulty.<\/h6>\n
Developing a learning mindset and essential soft skills cannot be an extra activity that is completed if time permits\u2014it must lay at the foundation of how teachers teach and how students learn.<\/h6>\n
We place a cardinal importance on the development of a learning mindset, giving it parallel importance to academic success.<\/strong><\/h5>\n

Reality-conscious<\/h1>\n

Equitable access to education is an issue that affects millions of students and their families, and private educational services are not usually known to be cost effective.<\/h6>\n
We design our services with the reality of budget-consciousness in mind, with simple terms of service. Our small-group sessions allow families to separate the cost of sessions without negatively impacting the quality of education.<\/h6>\n
We help families and teachers find educational support solutions for students in need.<\/strong> Our priority will always be to best position students to learn.<\/strong><\/h5>\n

Support public education<\/h1>\n

No tutoring service can ever replace school teachers \u2014 they are of unrivalled importance when it comes to a student\u2019s education. We know firsthand, however, that public school classrooms can\u2019t fully meet students\u2019 needs; too much gets in the way. All the more, funding cuts and the culture of deprofessionalization of educators has had profound effects on the quality of public education.<\/h6>\n
We directly support public education teachers and school programs via partnerships, program and supply grants, and curriculum-aligned resources.<\/strong><\/h5>\n

Ready for (re-)imagination<\/h1>\n

Public education too often enters a state of regression because of its self-prescribed need to innovate, using uninspiring initiatives derived from past objectives that do nothing to address the future of our learners.<\/h6>\n
Revolutions in public education, large or small, happen when it allows itself to transform; when memorization is replaced with understanding and application. When conformity and compliance are replaced with curiosity, creativity and imagination.<\/h6>\n
We design all our programs and services to be future-focused. Our objective is to give all learners the knowledge and skills to be ready for what is to come.<\/strong><\/h5>\n

For all modern advances and societal progress, one of the realms that requires much more attention is public education.<\/span> The way they are organized, public schools and classrooms simply can\u2019t meet our students\u2019 needs; too much gets in the way. Like in many parts of the world the people who make public education possible\u2014teachers, educators, school support staff\u2014in Ontario, Canada are often left to their own devices to fend off the threats to their students\u2019 learning potential.<\/span><\/p>\n

A culture of deprofessionalization presently plagues education workers, and has been recently fueled by political movements that aim to diminish the importance of quality education. Teachers report losses of autonomy and agency over their work, citing pressure from administrative forces that fail to consider the reality of the classroom.<\/p>\n

When teachers must become standardized curriculum implementors and classroom managers instead of creative pedagogues, students\u2019 propensity for learning suffers. Yet, that\u2019s precisely what current prescribed approaches are establishing.<\/p>\n

[\/et_pb_text][\/et_pb_column][et_pb_column type=”1_3″ _builder_version=”4.21.0″ _module_preset=”default” global_colors_info=”{}”][et_pb_text _builder_version=”4.21.0″ _module_preset=”default” text_text_color=”gcid-54d30d31-2298-4ebb-b2ab-7a5dd0e7c435″ global_colors_info=”{%22gcid-54d30d31-2298-4ebb-b2ab-7a5dd0e7c435%22:%91%22text_text_color%22%93}”]<\/p>\n

\u00a0Following funding cuts, schools are forced to reduce available aid to students due to low educators availability. Efforts to sufficiently support students have been diminished to sparse interventions.<\/p>\n

Initiatives developed by education leaders are too numerous, too complex and lack connection to classroom realities. An increasing overburden related to planning, documentation, reporting, meetings, convocations\u2026 consume both the time and emotional vitality that are needed for the daily task of teaching.<\/p>\n

Classrooms must act as havens for learning and flourishing. However, an increasing amount of obstacles interfere with teachers\u2019 abilities to create this safe environment for their students.<\/p>\n

Parents are referred to as partners in a child\u2019s education; the equilibrium in the relationship with their children\u2019s teachers are too often out of balance.<\/p>\n

Behavioural issues monopolize teachers\u2019 time and energy that is suppose to be dedicated to their craft and humanity. It has a profound impact on classroom and school culture, with lack of true accountability from liable students only serving to negatively impact others who deserve better.<\/p>\n
